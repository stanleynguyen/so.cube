Project SoCube

Kai Xiang solving rubik cube while juggling

Kai Xiang is a professional cuber who has broken multiple Singapore national records over his career. At his peak, he had 25 national records at the same time, but in a span of a few years, he lost all of them. He had always wondered how fast it would take for people to overtake him, it seemed to him that the cubing competition scene is very fast and the moments of being on top is very fleeting. As if to proof a point, before starting this project, he still had a record; currently he has none.

Thus, Project SoCube was born to answer the following questions: what is the journey of a professional cuber? Given historical data of a specific cuber and the results of cubing competitions all over the world, how does a professional cuber measure up to their peers at that time? Are there any geospatial patterns in a cuber’s career?

To answer these questions, we used data from  World’s Cube Association, or WCA for short. Since 2006, WCA has been the official record keeper and organizers for cubing competitions worldwide. Their data includes every registered competitors’ timing at various competitions, records broken at different competitions as well as the competition venue. It was perfect for our visualization that we intend to make – the timings of a professional cuber against a world map, over the course of his career as a monthly slider. To further answer our questions we also plot countries best timings to find out the top countries in speedcubing.

Our first choice for the tech stack is to use R and Python Pandas to wrangle the data while using Javascript with Baidu eCharts to visualize the data. However, we faced problems in using eCharts Chinese documentation so we finally used Leaflet instead. The frontend is served on surge.sh while the backend is a Heroku web application which uses our pre-processed data. In running this application, we also faced the problem of having not enough memory to process the incoming queries. While the breakpoints of the percentiles were pre-computed, we still have to fetch the best timing of the competitor thus far, which is taxing on the backend due to the huge amount of data to process.

We wrangled the data in a suitable format, by transforming the timings achieved at the various competitions into percentiles that can be compared with the cuber’s timing. We then plot the timings of the cuber into the percentile bins to see the cuber’s current position on a slider map. As time moves on, new techniques for solving the cube might be found, new cubers might rise and replace the old cubers, and the current position will usually drop. We can now visualize a cuber’s meteoric rise and fall in his career.

We choose a YlOrBr color scheme from colorbrewer2 as it is color blind friendly. There are Rubik's Cube for the colorblind hence we need to make sure it is usable for everyone. Besides, we chose our percentile for competitor vs countries to be very fine-grained to focus on the more competitive few rather than the all competitors.

Although Kai Xiang's records were all broken, his standings worldwide is still very good. Currently he is considered first percentile in most countries worldwide. Maybe a better visualization would be the percentiles of the record holders worldwide? For other cubers like Loh Jing Yen, his 3x3x3 score was 20th percentiles in Singapore but first in Indonesia, maybe he should went there to compete instead. In a sense, this tool is useful if you want to scout for a place to compete.

We did not get a coherent narative such as a rise in power by geographic region, instead what we got was a flare up of spots where the competitor's percentile improved or declined. Whle we originally thought that the rise and fall would be spread out by geographic area, the visualization proved us wrong.The fact that the colours keep shifting around tells us that spee dcubing is still an individual sport, without any big support from government or communities. The percentile changes mostly come from individuals who picked up the sport and excelled in it. Hence, there is no connections to geographic region and thus there is no change in colour by geographic region.

As for countries vs. countries, the visualization shows the top countries over the years as a shifting entity that never settles on any particular nation or region. In fact, different places have achieved first percentile over the years, including Finland, Netherlands, Poland, Japan and Australia, supporting our theory of speedcubing as an individual posrt. Furthermore, the visualization also showed us that China and Hungary, the place of origin of Rubik's Cube, were never top of the world, despite popular imagination.

So, hang around and play with the slider. If you are a WCA registered competitor, enter your name and see for yourself your timings against the world.
